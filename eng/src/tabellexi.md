<h2>Determiner Correlatives</h2>
<p>The correlatives words <strong>ke</strong>, <strong>hin</strong>, <strong>den</strong>, <strong>ban</strong>,
	<strong>moy</strong>, <strong>nil</strong>, <strong>alo</strong> and <strong>sama</strong> must always be followed
	by a <em>noun</em> (whether modified with adjectives or not) or by a <em>pronoun</em>. They should never stand alone
	because by omitting the <em>(pro)noun</em> the correlative can easily be mistaken as a determiner of the noun/verb
	that follows it. In the absence of a specified noun, the pronouns <strong>te</strong> or <strong>to</strong> mark
	the end of the noun phrase. See <a href="./jumlemonli-estrutur.html#pornamelexi_in_namelexili_jumlemon">Noun
		Phrases</a>.</p>
<p>Compare the following sentences:</p>
<p><strong>Hinto bon nasacu.</strong><br /> This (thing) smells good.</p>
<p>In the sentence above, <strong>-to</strong> marks the end of the noun phrase.</p>
<p><strong>Hin bon nasacu... memorigi mi cel misu femgami.</strong><br /> This good smell... reminds me of my wife.</p>
<p>In the sentence above, <strong>nasacu</strong> marks the end of the noun phrase.</p>
<h2><em>kekwanti</em>, <em>kenumer</em></h2>
<p>Likewise, <strong>kekwanti</strong> (what quantity <em>of</em>) and <strong>kenumer</strong> (what number
	<em>of</em>) must also be followed by <strong>te</strong> or <strong>to</strong> when a noun is understood and not
	specified.</p>
<p><strong>kenumer bon lala</strong> - how many good songs<br /> vs<br />
	<strong>Kenumer te bon lala?</strong><br /> How many (of them) sing well?
</p>
<p><strong>Mi le kari dua kilogramo fe risi. Yu le kari kekwanti to?</strong><br /> I bought two kilos of rice. How much
	did you buy?</p>
<h2>Obligatory <em>cel</em></h2>
<p>The preposition <strong>cel</strong> is obligatory with <strong>loka</strong> correlatives when movement is involved.
</p>
<p><strong>cel keloka</strong> - where (to)<br />
	<strong>cel hinloka</strong> - here (<em>hither</em>)<br />
	<strong>cel denloka</strong> - there (<em>thither</em>)<br /> etc.
</p>
<h2>Questions vs Interrogative Clauses within Declarative Sentences</h2>
<p><em>Interrogative clauses within declarative sentences</em> are clauses that appear in place of noun phrases and
	which mean <em>the answer to the question "XYZ?"</em> or a variation thereof. They are formed by introducing them
	with the clause conjunction <strong>ku</strong>, using the same determiner (<strong>ke</strong>) seen in true
	questions, and preserving the word order of the clause's counterpart question. </p>
<p>The following pairs of example sentences illustrate: (1) true questions, (2) declarative sentences with interrogative
	clauses</p>
<h3>ke - <em>which</em>; kete - <em>who(m)</em>; keto - <em>what</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Kete lubi yu?</strong><br /> "Who loves you?"<br /> Who loves you?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku kete lubi yu.</strong><br /> "I know this: <em>Who loves you?</em>."<br /> I know who loves
	you. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu lubi kete?</strong><br /> "You love who(m)."<br /> Who(m) do you love?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku yu lubi kete.</strong><br /> "I know this: <em>You love who(m)?</em>."<br /> I know who(m) you
	love. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Te vole na yam keto?</strong><br /> "He wants to eat what?"<br /> What does he want to eat?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi le wanji ku te vole na yam keto.</strong><br /> "I forgot this: <em>He wants to eat what?</em>."<br />
	I forgot what he wants to eat. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Te le gibe pesa tas ke doste?</strong><br /> "She gave the money to which friend?" Which friend did she
	give the money to? </p>
<p>(2) <strong>Te le no loga ku te le gibe pesa tas ke doste.</strong><br /> "She didn't say this <em>Which friend did
		she give the money to?</em>."<br /> She didn't say which friend she gave the money to. </p>
<h3>kesu - <em>whose</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Hinto sen kesu kursi?</strong><br /> "This is whose chair?"<br /> Whose chair is this?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi vole na jixi ku hinto sen kesu kursi.</strong><br /> "I want to know this: <em>This is whose
		chair?</em>."<br /> I want to know whose chair this is. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Kesu kitabu sen per mesa?</strong><br /> "Whose book on the table?"<br /> Whose book is on the table.</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku kesu kitabu sen per mesa.</strong><br /> "I know this: <em>Whose book is on the
		table?</em>."<br /> I know whose book is on the table. </p>
<h3>kepul - <em>like what or what kind of (with nouns); how (with verbs)</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu sen kepul?</strong><br /> "You are how?"<br /> How are you?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Te le swal ku yu sen kepul.</strong><br /> "She asked this: <em>You are how?</em>."<br /> She asked how
	you were. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu sen kepul insan?</strong><br /> "You are what kind of person?"<br /> What kind of person are you? </p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku yu sen kepul insan.</strong><br /> "I know this: <em>What kind of person are you?</em>."<br />
	I know what kind of person you are. </p>
<h3>kemo - <em>how (to what degree)</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Te sen kemo lao?</strong><br /> "She is how old?"<br /> How old is she?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Te le loga tas mi ku te sen kemo lao.</strong><br /> "She told me this: <em>She is how old?</em>."<br />
	She told me how old she is.</p>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu sen kemo pilodo?</strong><br /> "You are how tired?"<br /> How tired are you?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku yu sen kemo pilodo.</strong><br /> I know this: "You are how tired?".<br /> I know how tired
	you are. </p>
<h3>kekwanti - <em>how much</em>; kenumer - <em>how many</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu le kari kekwanti risi?</strong><br /> "You bought how much rice?"<br /> How much rice did you buy?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi le oko ku yu le kari kekwanti risi.</strong><br /> "I saw this: <em>You bought how much
		rice?</em>."<br /> I saw how much rice you bought. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu hare kenumer bete?</strong><br /> "You have how many children?"<br /> How many children do you have?
</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixipel ku yu hare kenumer bete.</strong><br /> "I wonder this: <em>You have how many
		children?</em>."<br /> I wonder how many children you have. </p>
<h3>keloka - <em>where</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Te ergo keloka?</strong><br /> "He works where?"<br /> Where does he work?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku te ergo keloka.</strong><br /> "I know this: <em>He works where?</em>."<br /> I know where he
	works. </p>
<h3>kewatu - <em>when</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Te xa preata kewatu?</strong><br /> "She will arrive when?"<br /> When will she arrive?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Dento sen ku te xa preata kewatu.</strong><br /> "That is this: <em>She will arrive when?</em>."<br />
	That is when she will arrive. </p>
<h3>keseba - <em>why</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu le no idi cel parti keseba?</strong><br /> "You didn't go to the party why?"<br /> Why didn't you go
	to the party?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixi ku yu le no idi cel parti keseba.</strong><br /> "I know this: <em>You didn't go to the party
		why?</em>."<br /> I know why you didn't go to the party. </p>
<h3>kemaner - <em>how (done how)</em></h3>
<p>(1) <strong>Yu le xuli mobil kemaner?</strong><br /> "You fixed the car how?"<br /> How did you fix the car?</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi jixipel ku yu le xuli mobil kemaner.</strong><br /> I wonder this: <em>You fixed the car
		how?</em>."<br /> I wonder how you fixed the car. </p>
<h2>Declarative Sentences with Interrogative Phrases</h2>
<p>Speakers will sometimes reduce an interrogative clause into a phrase, even to the lone interrogative word. The
	conjunction <strong>ku</strong> is still used in the absence of a full clause.</p>
<p><strong>Mi jixi ku fe ke mesi.</strong><br /> I know in what month.</p>
<p><strong>Te le no loga ku keseba.</strong><br /> She didn't say why.</p>
<p><strong>Dento sen ku keloka.</strong><br /> That is where. </p>
<p><strong>Mi jixi ku na idi keloka.</strong><br /> I know where to go.</p>
<h2>Correlative Phrasal Conjunctions</h2>
<p>Correlative phrasal conjunctions end in <strong>-loka</strong>, <strong>-watu</strong>, <strong>-seba</strong> and
	<strong>-maner</strong>, and use the relative conjunction <strong>hu</strong>. </p>
<p><strong>Mi ergo <em>denloka hu yu ergo</em>.</strong><br /> I work <em>where you work</em>.</p>
<p><strong>Mi xa preata <em>denwatu hu yam sen jumbi</em>.</strong> <em>or</em> <strong><em>Denwatu hu yam sen
			jumbi</em>, mi xa preata.</strong><br /> I will arrive <em>when the meal is ready</em>. <em>or</em> <em>When
		the meal is ready</em>, I will arrive.</p>
<p><strong>Mi le no idi cel parti <em>denseba hu yu idi</em>.</strong><br /> I didn't go to the party <em>for the reason
		that you went</em>.</p>
<p><strong>Mi le xuli mobil <em>denmaner hu yu le alim tas mi</em>.</strong><br /> I fixed the car <em>how/like you
		taught me</em>.</p>
<p><strong><em>Denmaner hu mi le loga...</em></strong><br />
	<em>Like I said</em>...
</p>
<h2>Comparative Correlatives</h2>
<p>The conjunction <strong>kom</strong> means <em>as, like</em> and is used with the correlatives ending in
	<strong>-pul</strong>, <strong>-mo</strong>, <strong>-kwanti</strong> and <strong>-numer</strong> to make
	comparisions. In the following pairs of example sentences, the second sentence replaces a specific word or phrase
	with a correlative. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Mi sen <em>hazuni kom yu</em>.</strong><br /> I am <em>sad like you</em>. </p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi sen <em>denpul kom yu</em>.</strong><br /> I am <em>like you</em>. </p>
<p>(1) <strong>Mi salom yu <em>sodarsim kom misu sodar</em>.</strong><br /> I greet you <em>fraternally as my
		brother</em>.</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi salom yu <em>denpul kom misu sodar</em>.</strong><br /> I greet you <em>as my brother</em>.</p>
<p>(1) <strong>Mi no abil na lala <em>meli kom yu</em>.</strong><br /> I can't sing <em>beautifully like you</em>.</p>
<p>(2) <strong>Mi no abil na lala <em>denpul kom yu</em>.</strong><br /> I can't sing <em>like you</em>.</p>
<p>(1) <strong><em>Sama kom mi,</em> pia te hare tiga bete.</strong><br />
	<em>The same as me,</em> she too has three children.
</p>
<p>(2) <strong><em>Denpul kom mi,</em> pia te hare tiga bete.</strong><br />
	<em>Like me,</em> she too has three children.
</p>
<p>(1) <strong>Hin baytu sen <em>daymo</em> day <em>kom misu to</em>.</strong><br /> This house is <em>very</em> big
	<em>like mine</em>. </p>
<p>(2) <strong>Hin baytu sen <em>denmo</em> day <em>kom misu to</em>.</strong><br /> This house is <em>as</em> big
	<em>as mine</em>. </p>

<h2>daydenpul</h2>
<p>The word <strong>daydenpul</strong> is a derived word composed of <strong>day-</strong> (<em>augmentative
		prefix</em>) and the correlative <strong>denpul</strong>. It translates as <em>what a</em> followed a noun in
	exclamations such as the following:</p>
<p><strong><em>Daydenpul</em> din!</strong><br />
	<em>What</em> a day!
</p>
<h2>daydenmo <a id="daydenmmo"></a></h2>
<p>The word <strong>daydenmo</strong> is an derived word composed of <strong>day-</strong> (<em>augmentative
		prefix</em>) and the correlative <strong>denmo</strong>. It is an adverb of degree meaning <em>so</em>, when
	followed by an adjective/adverb, or <em>such</em>, when followed by a modified noun. </p>
<p><strong>Yu <em>daydenmo</em> bala.</strong><br /> You are <em>so</em> strong.</p>
<p><strong>Yu hare <em>daydenmo</em> day oko.</strong><br /> You have <em>such</em> big eyes.</p>
<p>The word <strong>daydenmo</strong> is also used much like <strong>daydenpul</strong>. It means <em>how</em>, when
	followed by an adjective/adverb, or <em>what a</em>, when followed by a modified noun.</p>
<p><strong><em>Daydenmo</em> meli!</strong><br />
	<em>How</em> beautiful!
</p>
<p><strong><em>Daydenmo</em> meli din!</strong><br />
	<em>What a</em> beautiful day!
</p>
<h2>daydenkwanti, daydennumer, denmo multi</h2>
<p>Similarly, the words <strong>daydenkwanti</strong> and <strong>daydennumer</strong> may be used to express <em>so
		much</em> and <em>so many</em>, respectively. Alternatively, the expression <strong>denmo multi</strong> may be
	used to express either, as it is synonymous with both <strong>daydenkwanti</strong> and
	<strong>daydennumer</strong>.</p>
<h2>moyun</h2>
<p>The word <strong>moyun</strong> is a derived word composed of the correlative word <strong>moy</strong> and
	<strong>un</strong> (<em>one</em>). It means <em>each (one)</em> and is used when it is necessary to distinguish it
	from <em>every/all</em>.</p>
<p>Compare the following sentences:</p>
<p><strong>Mi le kari tiga yuxitul cel <em>moyun nini</em>.</strong><br /> I bought three toys for <em>each child</em>.
</p>
<p><strong>Mi le kari tiga yuxitul cel <em>moy nini</em>.</strong><br /> I bought three toys for <em>all the
		children</em>.</p>
